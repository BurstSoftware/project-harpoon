Markets are not operating effectively

Thoughts

Markets often face challenges, but they generally operate more effectively than centralized alternatives.

The Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH) posits that asset prices reflect all available information, making it hard to consistently "beat the market." In practice, markets show semi-strong or weak efficiency most of the time: prices adjust rapidly to news, arbitrage opportunities fade quickly, and capital flows toward higher-return uses. However, they're not perfect—bubbles, crashes, behavioral biases, information asymmetries, and policy distortions create temporary or structural inefficiencies.Current Context (Mid-2026)Equity markets have shown resilience this year despite headwinds:S&P 500 up ~8% YTD as of mid-June.

Under the surface, there's concentration: a small number of stocks (tech/AI leaders) drive indexes, while many others lag. Valuations are elevated in places, consumer pressures exist, and inflation/geopolitics add volatility. Yet markets have climbed a "wall of worry," rewarding risk-takers. This isn't failure—it's price discovery in action amid uncertainty.

Why Inefficiencies AriseBehavioral factors: Herding, over-optimism (e.g., AI hype), or panic.
External interventions: Central bank policies, regulations, subsidies, or trade barriers distort signals. Examples include prolonged low rates fueling asset bubbles or energy market shocks from conflicts.
Structural issues: High-frequency trading, dark pools, or illiquidity in certain assets can amplify moves. Concentration in indices or mega-caps reduces breadth.
Information gaps: In emerging sectors or private markets, asymmetries persist.

Real-world evidence (e.g., persistent alpha from skilled managers, value anomalies, or momentum effects) challenges strong-form EMH. Markets aren't omniscient, but they aggregate dispersed knowledge better than any planner—Hayek's "knowledge problem" in action. Failures like 2008 or dot-com often trace back to distorted incentives (e.g., moral hazard from bailouts or easy money).Evidence of EffectivenessCapital allocation: Innovation booms (AI spending cycles) show markets directing resources to high-potential areas.
