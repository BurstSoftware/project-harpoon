Housing market, rating agencies, government, banks

Thoughts

The interplay between the housing market, credit rating agencies, government, and banks has been central to major financial events, especially the 2008 crisis, and continues to shape mortgage finance today. 

en.wikipedia.org

Historical Context: The 2008 Financial CrisisIn the years leading to 2008, loose lending standards, a housing price bubble, and securitization of mortgages created systemic risks. Key dynamics included:Government Role: Policies pushed for broader homeownership, including through Government-Sponsored Enterprises (GSEs) like Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac. These bought mortgages from lenders, securitized them into mortgage-backed securities (MBS), and provided liquidity. Political pressure for "affordable housing" led them to expand into riskier loans in the mid-2000s, though they largely followed rather than led the subprime surge. GSEs held or guaranteed a large portion of U.S. mortgages and were placed in conservatorship in 2008 at huge taxpayer cost. 

en.wikipedia.org

Banks and Lenders: Commercial and investment banks originated subprime and Alt-A loans with low documentation, low/no down payments, and adjustable rates. They bundled these into MBS and collateralized debt obligations (CDOs), selling them to investors. This "originate-to-distribute" model shifted risk off bank balance sheets but amplified leverage and opacity. Investment banks like Lehman Brothers held large exposures. 

en.wikipedia.org

Rating Agencies (Moody's, S&P, Fitch): Agencies assigned high ratings (often AAA) to complex structured products backed by risky subprime mortgages. This was driven by the "issuer-pays" model (issuers paid for ratings), competition for market share, flawed models assuming continued house price rises, and limited historical data on subprime performance. These ratings enabled pension funds, banks, and others to buy the securities and helped meet regulatory capital requirements. Massive downgrades in 2007–2008 triggered panic selling and liquidity crises. 

stern.nyu.edu

The result: A housing bubble burst, mass foreclosures, devalued securities, bank failures/bailouts, and the Great Recession. The Financial Crisis Inquiry Commission highlighted failures across all parties—misaligned incentives, poor risk management, regulatory gaps, and over-reliance on ratings. 

govinfo.gov

Post-crisis reforms (e.g., Dodd-Frank) increased oversight of rating agencies, reduced some regulatory reliance on ratings, and tightened mortgage standards, but GSEs remain in conservatorship with ongoing debates about their future.Current Housing Market (as of mid-2026)The market differs from the pre-2008 boom: Higher interest rates (around 6%+ for 30-year fixed), elevated home prices, and affordability challenges dominate. 

usbank.com

Government Influence: Fannie Mae, Freddie Mac, and Ginnie Mae still back a massive share of mortgages and securities (agency MBS market >$9 trillion). Nonbank lenders have grown, with federal entities like FHFA and Ginnie Mae monitoring risks. Policies affect affordability via guarantees, tax incentives, and regulations. 

gao.gov

Banks: They hold residential loans with generally sound credit quality, though some see rising delinquencies in certain portfolios. Lending standards are tighter than pre-2008, with focus on capital, liquidity, and interest rate risk. Banks compete with nonbanks. 

fdic.gov

Rating Agencies: They rate MBS and related instruments more conservatively post-crisis, with greater scrutiny and competition. Their opinions still influence investor demand and bank capital rules, though reliance has been tempered.

House prices have slowed or stalled in many areas amid high rates and inventory increases, with regional variations. New construction and incentives help, but affordability remains strained (e.g., high prices relative to incomes in places like California). 

jpmorgan.com

Ongoing Interconnections and RisksSecuritization Pipeline: Banks/lenders originate loans → GSEs or private issuers securitize → Rating agencies assess → Investors (including banks) buy. Government backing lowers costs for conforming loans.
Incentives and Conflicts: Issuer-pays ratings, government implicit guarantees, and profit motives can still distort risk assessment.
Systemic Links: Downgrades or housing stress can hit bank balance sheets, trigger funding issues, or require government intervention. Shadow banking/nonbanks add complexity. 

som.yale.edu

Lessons from 2008 emphasize transparency, better risk modeling, diversified information sources beyond ratings, and balanced policy. The system is more resilient but not immune to rate shocks, policy shifts, or economic downturns. For the latest data, check sources like FHFA, Federal Reserve, or MBA reports.
