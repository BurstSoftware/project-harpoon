DXY (also known as the US Dollar Index, USDX, or "Dixie") is a measure of the value of the United States dollar relative to a basket of six major foreign currencies. 

en.wikipedia.org

It serves as a benchmark for the overall strength or weakness of the USD in global forex markets. The index rises when the dollar strengthens against the basket and falls when it weakens.How It's CalculatedDXY is a weighted geometric mean of exchange rates against these currencies (weights have remained largely unchanged since the euro's introduction in 1999): 

en.wikipedia.org

Euro (EUR): 57.6%
Japanese yen (JPY): 13.6%
British pound (GBP): 11.9%
Canadian dollar (CAD): 9.1%
Swedish krona (SEK): 4.2%
Swiss franc (CHF): 3.6%

The euro dominates the basket because it represents a large portion of U.S. trade.History and Base ValueLaunched in 1973 by the U.S. Federal Reserve after the Bretton Woods system ended (now maintained by ICE).
Base value set at 100 (March 1973). Values above 100 indicate the dollar is stronger than in 1973; below 100 means weaker.

Why DXY MattersForex trading: Traders use it to gauge USD strength, which impacts currency pairs (e.g., a rising DXY often pressures EUR/USD lower).
Global markets: A stronger dollar can make U.S. exports more expensive, affect commodity prices (often priced in USD), and influence emerging markets with dollar-denominated debt.
Economic indicator: It reflects U.S. monetary policy (e.g., Fed rate changes), inflation, and global risk sentiment.

You can track it live on sites like TradingView (TVC:DXY), Yahoo Finance (DX-Y.NYB), or MarketWatch. Note that DXY doesn't include all trading partners (e.g., no Chinese yuan or Mexican peso), so it's not a perfect broad trade-weighted measure.
