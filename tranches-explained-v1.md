It would be the senior (AAA-rated) tranche of a subprime or deep-subprime mortgage-backed security (MBS/RMBS) or a collateralized debt obligation (CDO) backed by such loans. 

en.wikipedia.org

This matches the classic structured finance products (especially from the mid-2000s housing boom) that used tranching and subordination to create high credit ratings from risky underlying assets.Key Elements of Your DescriptionAAA (triple-A) rating on the bond/tranche: Rating agencies (Moody’s, S&P, Fitch) assigned their highest rating to the most senior portions of these deals. The senior tranche gets paid first from cash flows (interest and principal) from the entire pool of loans. Lower tranches (mezzanine, equity/junior) absorb losses first, providing a “buffer” or subordination that protects the senior piece. 

bis.org

“Up to 65% of the bond being [the] AAA [portion]”: This refers to the size of the senior AAA tranche(s) within the overall capital structure. In many subprime MBS and especially CDOs, 60–80%+ of the issued securities were rated AAA (sometimes split into “super-senior” and junior AAA). A 65% AAA slice fits comfortably within historical structures. The remaining ~35% consisted of lower-rated or unrated tranches that provided the credit enhancement. 

en.wikipedia.org

Underlying collateral with 95% FICO scores below 550: FICO scores below 550 fall into the “very poor” or deep-subprime category (typical subprime lending targeted scores below ~620, with averages often in the low-to-mid 600s in the mid-2000s). A pool where 95% of borrowers have scores this low would be extraordinarily risky—far worse than standard subprime pools of the era—with very high expected default rates. 

consumerfinance.gov

Rating agencies often focused on average FICO scores in the pool (e.g., around 615 as a rough threshold for allowing large AAA portions) rather than the full distribution or tail risk. High concentrations of very low scores increase default correlation risk, especially in a housing downturn. 

en.wikipedia.org

How This Structure Worked (and Why It Was Problematic)The pool of loans (mostly residential mortgages) generates cash flows. These are sliced into tranches with different priorities and risk levels:Senior AAA tranche(s) — e.g., up to ~65% of the deal — paid first, protected by subordination, rated AAA.
Mezzanine tranches (AA down to BBB).
Equity/junior tranche — absorbs first losses, often unrated or low-rated, held by the issuer or sophisticated investors.

This “financial engineering” allowed issuers to take a pool of low-quality loans and sell most of it as supposedly ultra-safe AAA paper to investors (pensions, insurers, banks, etc.) who were restricted to high-rated securities. The AAA tranches offered slightly higher yields than plain corporate or government AAA bonds, which made them attractive. 

investopedia.com

In reality, when home prices fell and defaults surged (far beyond model assumptions of low correlation), even many senior AAA tranches suffered losses or massive downgrades. Some studies later showed that AAA subprime tranches ultimately performed better than lower-rated ones in the non-agency RMBS market, but the overall system was destabilized by leverage, interconnected CDOs (“CDO-squared”), and over-reliance on flawed ratings/models. 

nber.org

Bottom LineSuch a “bond” is not a plain vanilla corporate or government bond. It is a structured finance security — specifically the protected senior slice of a securitization of very poor-credit-quality mortgages. The AAA rating reflected the structure and subordination assumptions more than the inherent quality of the 95% deep-subprime loans underneath.These products were central to the 2007–2008 financial crisis. Post-crisis regulations (Dodd-Frank, risk retention rules, etc.) made such aggressive tranching and rating of subprime-heavy pools much harder and less common, though tranching and securitization still exist in safer forms today (e.g., prime auto loans, credit cards, or high-quality mortgages).If you have more context (e.g., a specific deal name, year, or whether this is about investing risk/yield today), I can refine the explanation further.
