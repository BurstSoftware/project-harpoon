Why is the market for insuring the bonds 20x the bond market

Thoughts

The "market for insuring the bonds" primarily refers to the credit default swap (CDS) market (and broader credit derivatives), where participants buy/sell protection against bond defaults. CDS contracts function like insurance: the buyer pays a periodic premium to the seller, who compensates them (typically the difference between par value and recovery value) if the reference bond or entity defaults. 

en.wikipedia.org

This market's notional value (the face amount of debt the contracts reference) has historically been many times larger than the actual outstanding bond (or loan) market—often 5–10x or more for corporates overall, and far higher for individual names. The "20x" figure is likely an approximation or reference to peak ratios, specific entities, or gross exposure in older data (e.g., pre- or post-2008 crisis commentary). 

princeton.edu +1

Why the CDS/"Insurance" Market Is Much Larger Than the Bond MarketSynthetic and Unlimited Creation (Naked CDS):
Unlike actual bonds, which have a fixed supply issued by the borrower, CDS are derivatives. You do not need to own the underlying bond to buy or sell protection. "Naked" CDS (speculative positions without owning the debt) allow anyone to bet on default risk. There is no natural limit on how many contracts can be written on the same reference entity. 

en.wikipedia.org

  Result: For many companies, CDS notional has greatly exceeded their actual debt outstanding (e.g., ratios >100% gross notional common for corporates; in extremes like certain cases, much higher multiples). 

iosco.org

Hedging + Speculation + Intermediation:  Hedging: Bondholders (e.g., banks, insurers) buy CDS protection. But dealers and others create offsetting trades.  
Speculation: Traders bet on credit spreads widening/tightening or defaults without owning bonds (far easier and more liquid than shorting illiquid bonds).  
Market making: Dealers run books with gross positions that net out but inflate total notional. Gross notional measures counterparty risk exposure; net notional (after offsets) is much smaller and closer to actual risk transfer. 

iosco.org

Liquidity and Structural Advantages of CDS Over Bonds:
Corporate bonds are often illiquid (buy-and-hold investors, OTC trading, large denominations). CDS trade more easily, with lower transaction costs and standardized terms, attracting more activity. This diverts trading/price discovery to CDS while amplifying notional relative to the thinner cash bond market. 

financetheory.org +1

Historical Scale:  Peak: ~$62 trillion gross notional in 2007 (vs. global corporate/government bonds in the tens of trillions).  
Post-crisis: Compression, clearing, and regulation reduced gross notional (e.g., to ~$10 trillion range in recent years), but it still often exceeds underlying debt multiples for corporates. U.S. CDS alone has been in the trillions recently. 

en.wikipedia.org +1

Global bonds outstanding are now ~$130–145 trillion (including sovereigns, corporates, etc.), with CDS a fraction but still disproportionately large on a per-name basis. 

icmagroup.org

Key Caveats on "Size"Gross vs. Net: Gross notional overstates risk due to offsetting contracts (e.g., A sells protection to B, who sells to C). Net notional is far lower and better reflects economic exposure. 

iosco.org

Not All "Insurance" Pays Out Equally: Physical settlement (delivering bonds) or cash settlement limits real payouts. Auctions handle cases where CDS notional >> deliverable bonds. 

princeton.edu

Regulation Impact: Post-2008 reforms (central clearing, trade compression, reporting) reduced gross sizes and systemic risks compared to the 2008 peak.

In short, the CDS market is vastly larger because it's a flexible, synthetic way to trade credit risk independently of the limited supply of actual bonds—combining genuine hedging with widespread speculation and dealer activity. This multiplier effect is inherent to derivatives markets. For the latest figures, sources like BIS, OCC, or ISDA provide ongoing data.
