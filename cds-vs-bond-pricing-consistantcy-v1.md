Pricing the swaps like the bond is going to fail but the price of the bond went up

Thoughts

This sounds like a reference to Credit Default Swaps (CDS) on a corporate or sovereign bond. CDS are essentially insurance contracts against a bond issuer defaulting ("failing"). The pricing of the CDS reflects the market's view of default risk, while the bond's cash market price reflects both credit risk and other factors like interest rates. There's no inherent contradiction when the bond price rises—the two markets tend to move consistently, with the CDS adjusting to the improved outlook. 

en.wikipedia.org

Quick MechanicsCDS pricing: The buyer of protection pays a periodic premium (the CDS spread, in basis points per year) to the seller. In return, if the reference bond defaults, the seller pays the buyer (typically par value minus the post-default recovery value of the bond).
The spread is set so the contract has near-zero value at inception. It is driven primarily by:Probability of default (PD).
Loss given default (LGD = 1 - recovery rate).
Time to maturity, interest rates, etc.

Roughly: CDS spread ≈ (PD × LGD) annualized, adjusted for discounting and other factors. Higher perceived risk → wider spread (more expensive protection).

Bond price: Moves inversely with its yield. If investors see lower default risk, they pay more for the bond (price ↑ → yield/spread ↓).

Why the Bond Price Can Rise While CDS "Prices Like Failure"CDS do not assume the bond will definitely fail. They price the expected cost of a possible failure (risk-neutral probability). If new information makes default less likely:Bond price rises (investors demand less credit risk premium).
CDS spread tightens (narrows), making protection cheaper. 

en.wikipedia.org

This is the normal, consistent relationship. A rising bond price signals improving creditworthiness, so the "insurance" (CDS) becomes less valuable/expensive. The opposite also holds: worsening outlook → bond price falls, CDS spread widens. 

en.wikipedia.org

No paradox—the CDS market is just quantifying the residual risk. Markets can diverge temporarily due to liquidity differences (CDS often more liquid for hedging/speculation), supply/demand imbalances, counterparty concerns, or technical factors like cheapest-to-deliver options in CDS auctions. This is called the CDS-bond basis. But arbitrage (e.g., negative basis trades: buy bond + buy CDS protection) tends to keep them aligned over time. 

princeton.edu +1

ExampleSuppose a company's 5-year bond trades at a wide spread (high yield, low price) because default looks possible.
Good news hits (strong earnings, debt reduction): Bond price jumps up.
CDS spread on the same name tightens sharply because the probability-weighted expected payout on the swap drops. Protection buyers pay less premium.

If the bond price rose due to falling interest rates (not credit improvement), the CDS spread might not move much, since CDS are more pure credit instruments (less sensitive to rates than fixed-rate bonds).

Other "Swaps" Context?If this refers to interest rate swaps (not credit), the logic differs: Swaps exchange fixed vs. floating rates and don't directly price "bond failure." Bond prices and swap rates both react to rates/credit, but a rising bond price (falling yields) would typically lower swap rates too. Less likely the intent here given "fail."For precise valuation, models like Hull-White or reduced-form credit models use bond prices/recovery assumptions to back out implied default probabilities for CDS.
