Thoughts

Yes, in the context of structured mortgage-backed securities (MBS), particularly private-label or non-agency ones and commercial mortgage-backed securities (CMBS), mortgage bonds are often structured as a "capital stack" of tranches with ratings ranging from AAA (senior) down to B or lower (subordinated/mezzanine/junior), plus sometimes unrated equity.

Key ConceptsMortgage bonds typically refer to mortgage-backed securities (MBS) or bonds secured by pools of mortgages (residential or commercial). These are not single loans but securitized products where cash flows from many underlying mortgages are passed through or allocated to investors. 

en.wikipedia.org

In pass-through MBS (common for agency securities like those from Fannie Mae or Freddie Mac), investors share pro-rata in payments without heavy tranching, and senior portions are often implicitly high-quality (AAA-like) due to government guarantees. However, the question aligns more with tranched structures.

How Tranching WorksIn Collateralized Mortgage Obligations (CMOs) or structured MBS/CMBS:A pool of mortgages is placed into a trust.
This pool is divided into tranches (slices) with different priorities for receiving principal and interest payments (a "waterfall" structure).
Senior tranches (e.g., AAA) get paid first and absorb losses last → lowest risk, lowest yield.
Mezzanine/subordinated tranches (AA down to BBB) have more risk.
Junior tranches (BB to B or lower, including unrated) absorb losses first → highest risk, highest potential yield.

If the previous bonds were made up of AAA to B and public but the modern ones are private and made up of AAA to C or below, what changes

Thoughts

The key shift is from predominantly government-backed (agency/public) MBS with implicit or explicit guarantees and limited credit tranching, to private-label (non-agency) MBS that rely entirely on internal structural protections (subordination/waterfall) and thus feature deeper or more explicit credit tranching down to lower ratings like BB, B, or even unrated/CCC-equivalent pieces. 

investopedia.com

This isn't always a strict "AAA to B" vs. "AAA to C or below" divide—both can include a range—but the nature, risk allocation, and market dynamics have changed significantly, especially post-2008 financial crisis. 

newyorkfed.org

Main Changes in Structure and RiskCredit Risk Bearing: Agency (public/previous dominant): Backed by Fannie Mae, Freddie Mac, or Ginnie Mae. Minimal true credit risk for investors (government guarantee or implicit support). Tranching is mainly for prepayment/timing risk (e.g., CMOs with sequential pay classes), not credit losses. Often treated as AAA-like without formal ratings.

Private-label (modern non-agency): No government guarantee. All credit risk is borne by investors via subordination. Senior AAA tranches are protected by junior pieces absorbing losses first (waterfall structure). Deals often include AAA (super senior/junior senior), AA/A, BBB (mezzanine), BB/B (sub-investment grade "B-pieces"), and unrated equity/first-loss. Lower tranches can go into speculative-grade territory, including deeper subordination for riskier collateral.

Tranche Depth and Ratings:Private deals create AAA from pools that might include jumbo, Alt-A, or other non-conforming loans by over-collateralizing seniors (e.g., 65–90%+ AAA in some structures, with 10–35% subordination). Junior pieces (BB to unrated) provide the buffer and can be more "C-like" or distressed in stress scenarios.

Lower-rated bonds (e.g., BB, B, or lower/unrated "B-pieces" or equity tranches) in mortgage-backed securities (MBS) or CMBS are the subordinated/junior portions of the capital stack. They sit at the opposite end of the risk-return spectrum from AAA senior tranches.

Core Differences (Waterfall/Priority Structure)Payment Priority: AAA (senior) tranches receive principal and interest first from the pool's cash flows (mortgage payments).
Lower-rated tranches (mezzanine BB/BBB down to junior B or unrated) get paid only after all senior tranches are current. They are last in line for distributions.

What happens when Bs go from a little risky to dog shit

Thoughts

When B-rated (or lower) junior/subordinated tranches in MBS or CMBS deteriorate from mildly risky to severely impaired ("dog shit"), they absorb the brunt of losses first, often resulting in missed payments, principal write-downs, massive value drops, or total wipeouts—while senior tranches (AAA and higher investment-grade) remain protected until the junior buffer is exhausted. 

investmentmanagement.prudential.com

Mechanics in a Deteriorating ScenarioLoss Allocation (Waterfall Hits Bottom First):Defaults, foreclosures, or shortfalls on underlying mortgages generate realized losses.
These hit the unrated equity/first-loss piece first → then BB → then B-rated tranches (and any lower like CCC or unrated).
B tranches stop receiving interest/principal once losses reach their level. Principal is written down sequentially from the bottom up. 

investmentmanagement.prudential.com

Immediate Effects on B Holders:Cash Flow Disruption: Interest payments get deferred or eliminated; principal repayments halt.
Principal Erosion: Losses directly reduce the tranche's outstanding balance. In severe cases (e.g., widespread defaults exceeding subordination), the entire B tranche can be wiped out.
Rating Downgrades: Agencies slash ratings (e.g., B to CCC, C, or D), reflecting higher expected losses. 

nber.org

Market and Liquidity Impact:Market value plummets (often trading at deep discounts or near zero in distress).
Liquidity evaporates—hard to sell without huge haircuts.
Higher yields demanded by any remaining buyers, but trading becomes distressed/debt-like.

Special Role in CMBS (B-Pieces)B-pieces (BB/B and below, often bought as a block) are the "controlling class." Holders get:Greater access to information.
Influence over special servicing and loan workouts (e.g., modify loans, foreclose, or extend).

In bad scenarios, this gives them some control to maximize recovery—but their first-loss position means they bear most pain. They may push aggressive workouts if it helps them, even if it conflicts with senior holders.

Deal Triggers: May activate performance triggers (e.g., faster paydown to seniors, trapping cash).
Investor Behavior: Hedge funds/specialized buyers (who seek high yields) hold or buy distressed B pieces at bargain prices, betting on recovery. Conservative investors avoid or sell early.
Historical Example (2008 Crisis): Junior tranches in private-label RMBS/CMBS suffered enormous losses (often 50–100%+), while many AAA held up better than feared—but overall market froze. Post-crisis structures have thicker subordination and better collateral, so juniors are more isolated.

When the Bs have underwriting like: rock bottom fico scores, no income verification, and adjustable rates

Thoughts

When the underlying mortgage pool feeding a securitization has poor underwriting—rock-bottom FICO scores (often <620–660), no/low income verification (e.g., NINJA, stated-income, or no-doc loans), and adjustable-rate mortgages (ARMs, especially hybrid teasers that reset higher)—the B-rated (and lower) junior tranches face dramatically amplified risk and are typically the first to suffer severe, often total, losses. 

newyorkfed.org

This setup mirrors the pre-2008 subprime and Alt-A private-label MBS boom, where such features drove high default rates once housing prices stalled and rates reset.Why This Underwriting Is Toxic for JuniorsHigh Default Probability: Low-FICO borrowers already have weak credit histories. No income verification hides inability to repay (liar loans). ARMs with low initial "teaser" rates create payment shocks on reset (e.g., +25–100%+ jumps), especially if LIBOR rises or home values drop, preventing refinancing. 

newyorkfed.org

Correlated Losses: These loans perform fine in rising housing markets (borrowers refinance or sell). When conditions worsen (price drops, unemployment, rate hikes), defaults spike together across the pool → losses exceed expected levels used in structuring.

Junior Position Amplifies It: B tranches (BB/B or below) absorb losses first via the waterfall. Even moderate pool-level defaults (e.g., 10–20%+) can wipe out subordination buffers for juniors. In extreme cases (like 2006–2008 vintages), losses far exceeded that.
