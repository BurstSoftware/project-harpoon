What is a short position against a stock or bond

Thoughts

A short position (also called short selling or being "short") against a stock or bond is a trading strategy where you bet that the price of the security will decline. You profit if the price falls, but you lose (potentially a lot) if it rises. 

investopedia.com

How It Works (Step by Step)Borrow the security: You borrow shares of a stock (or bonds) from your broker (who borrows them from another investor or institution).
Sell it immediately: You sell the borrowed shares/bonds on the open market at the current price. The proceeds from this sale go into your account.
Wait and hope for a price drop.
Buy it back (cover the short): Later, you purchase the same number of shares/bonds at (hopefully) a lower price and return them to the lender (via your broker).
Profit or loss: Your profit = Sale price - Buyback price (minus fees, interest, dividends, etc.). If the buyback price is higher, you lose money. 

schwab.com

This requires a margin account (not a regular cash account) because you're borrowing the asset.Simple Example: Shorting a StockA stock is trading at $50 per share.
You borrow 100 shares and sell them immediately → You receive $5,000.
Later, the price drops to $40.
You buy back 100 shares for $4,000 and return them.
Profit: $1,000 (minus borrowing fees, commissions, etc.). 

investopedia.com

If the price instead rises to $60, you'd lose $1,000+ to buy them back.Shorting BondsThe process is similar:You borrow bonds and sell them.
You must pay the lender any interest (coupons) that accrues while the position is open.
Bonds can be shorted for various reasons (e.g., expecting rising interest rates, which lower bond prices, or credit concerns).
It's often done via bond futures or ETFs for retail traders, as direct bond borrowing can be more complex. 

investopedia.com

Key Differences: Stocks vs. BondsStocks: More common for retail traders. Unlimited upside risk (stock price can rise indefinitely).
Bonds: Price moves are often more predictable (tied to interest rates, credit risk). You pay ongoing interest to the lender.

Risks of a Short PositionUnlimited losses on stocks (price can keep rising).
Margin calls: If the price moves against you, your broker may demand more money or force you to close the position.
Short squeeze: When many shorts cover at once, driving the price even higher (e.g., GameStop in 2021).
Borrowing costs/fees, dividends (you pay them on shorted stocks), and potential inability to borrow the security ("hard to borrow").
It's considered an advanced, high-risk strategy.

Opposite of a long position: Going long means you own the asset and profit if the price rises.Short positions are used by hedge funds, speculators, and for hedging (protecting other investments).
