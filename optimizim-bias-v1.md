People hate to think about bad things happening, so they underestimate the likelihood

That's a classic description of several related cognitive biases.People do tend to underestimate the probability of unpleasant events, largely because dwelling on them feels bad. This isn't just "positive thinking"—it's wired into how our brains handle uncertainty and emotion. Here's a breakdown of the key mechanisms:Main Biases at PlayOptimism Bias: We systematically believe bad things are less likely to happen to us specifically. Studies show people underestimate their personal risk of divorce, job loss, illness, car accidents, or climate impacts, even when they accurately assess risks for others. A famous meta-analysis found this bias is robust across cultures and ages.
Normalcy Bias: The assumption that "things will continue as they always have." It leads people to downplay warnings about disasters (e.g., ignoring evacuation orders during hurricanes or financial crashes) because imagining collapse is psychologically costly.
Availability Heuristic (with a negative twist): We judge likelihood by how easily examples come to mind. Rare but vivid bad events (plane crashes) get overweighted, while gradual or abstract ones (slow-building health issues, cyber risks, pension shortfalls) get underweighted because they're hard to picture or emotionally draining to rehearse.
Probability Neglect: When emotions run high, people focus on the severity of an outcome rather than its actual odds. This can flip: for low-grade dread (e.g., "something bad might happen"), we just avoid thinking about it altogether, leading to under-preparation.

Real-World ExamplesInsurance & Preparedness: Many skip flood insurance or backups until after a disaster. Post-event, demand spikes—classic availability + normalcy.
Personal Finance: Under-saving for retirement or emergencies because "I'll be fine" feels better than modeling worst-case scenarios.
Public Policy: Societies often under-invest in pandemic preparedness, infrastructure resilience, or existential risks until something hits.
Relationships/Health: Smokers or people in bad habits underestimate their odds of consequences, despite knowing the base rates.

Why Brains Do ThisEvolution favored energy-efficient thinking. Constantly simulating disasters would be paralyzing and demotivating. Mild optimism correlates with better mental health and persistence. But in modern environments with low-frequency, high-impact risks (black swans, tail risks), this becomes maladaptive.Counter-strategies that work reasonably well:Pre-mortems: Imagine a project/future failed, then work backward to why. Forces consideration of negatives without emotional shutdown.
Reference Class Forecasting: Ignore your gut "it'll be different for me" and look at base rates from similar past cases.
Explicit Probability Training: People get better with practice (e.g., prediction markets, calibration exercises).
"Defensive Pessimism": Some individuals harness anxiety productively by planning for the worst while hoping for the best.
