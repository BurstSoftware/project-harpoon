That's an insightful observation on human psychology and how we navigate values, trust, and decision-making. Let me rephrase and unpack it for clarity, then explore why it rings true (with some nuances).Rephrased versionPeople crave external authority to guide their valuations—what's worth pursuing, what's "good," what to prioritize in life, ethics, status, or daily choices. Yet when selecting which authority to follow, they don't purely defer blindly. Instead, they filter authorities through two main lenses:Competence signals: Observable facts, results, track records, or demonstrated expertise.
Familiarity: Comfort with the source—cultural closeness, similarity, repeated exposure, or emotional resonance—often trumping raw evidence.

In short: We want someone (or something) to outsource valuation to, but we pick our gurus based on a mix of "this works" and "this feels like home."Why this dynamic existsValuation is hard and costly. Assigning worth to careers, relationships, political systems, moral rules, or consumer goods requires massive information processing, foresight, and emotional labor. Evolution wired us for efficiency: defer to authorities rather than reinvent the wheel. Religions, ideologies, experts, influencers, traditions, and even algorithms fill this role. Without them, paralysis or poor choices ensue.
Choosing authority via results/facts (epistemic rationality). We track outcomes: "This doctor's patients recover more," "This investing strategy beat the market," "This policy reduced crime in similar places." 
Science, markets, sports, and engineering reward this. Successful authorities earn trust through predictive power or verifiable wins. People switch doctors, switch apps, or abandon failed gurus when results disappoint.

Choosing via familiarity (psychological and social shortcuts).Familiarity breeds perceived validity (the "mere exposure effect"). A teacher who speaks your dialect, a leader from your cultural background, or an influencer who shares your aesthetic feels more trustworthy—even if their track record is weaker.
This overlaps with in-group bias, authority bias, and halo effects. A familiar priest or pundit can shape your values more than a statistically superior but alien expert. Tribal loyalty and social proof amplify it: "My people endorse this."
Emotional comfort matters. Abstract "best" authority often loses to one that aligns with identity or reduces anxiety.

These two criteria clash often. A results-driven outsider (e.g., a foreign economist with superior data) may lose to a charismatic local familiar face. Conversely, when stakes are high and measurable (surgery, engineering failure), competence usually wins out.Real-world examplesPolitics and media: Voters often want parties/leaders to define national "values" (freedom, equality, security). They pick based on past results plus familiarity—shared language, memes, class, ethnicity, or media ecosystem. Polls show trust correlates heavily with "feels like one of us."
Self-help and wellness: Gurus promise valuation frameworks ("hustle culture," mindfulness, stoicism). Followers flock to those with visible success and relatable stories. A familiar YouTuber beats a drier academic book.
Markets and tech: Consumers choose brands not just on specs but familiarity (Apple's ecosystem loyalty despite competitors' benchmarks). Yet pure results drive switches in high-information domains like software tools.
Religion and tradition: Deep valuation (meaning of life, morality) often defaults to familiar inherited systems over novel but evidence-based philosophies—unless crisis forces a results-based reevaluation.

Limitations and caveatsThis isn't universal or fixed. Education, openness to experience, and high-stakes accountability shift weight toward facts/results. Institutions (peer review, markets, legal systems) try to institutionalize competence over familiarity. But humans remain bounded: familiarity is a cheap heuristic that worked in ancestral small tribes. In global, data-rich environments, it creates echo chambers, status quo bias, and resistance to better ideas.Critically, authorities themselves compete on both axes. Charismatic ones exploit familiarity; rigorous ones lean on results. The tension explains cultural evolution: successful societies blend both—familiar rituals + evidence-based updates.Your point highlights a core human frailty and strength: we're not pure rational calculators nor blank slates for any authority.
