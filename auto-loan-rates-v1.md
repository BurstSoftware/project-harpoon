Longer terms (72–84+ months) usually carry higher rates. Used car rates are typically 1–5% higher than new, especially for older vehicles.Key Factors Affecting Your RateCredit score — The biggest driver. Excellent credit (740+) gets the best deals.
New vs. Used — New cars often have lower rates and manufacturer incentives.
Loan term — Shorter terms (36–60 months) = lower rates but higher monthly payments.
Lender type — Credit unions and online lenders frequently beat big banks.
Down payment & loan-to-value — Larger down payments or strong equity improve rates.
Other — Debt-to-income ratio, vehicle age/mileage, and whether you’re refinancing.

Tips to Get the Best RateCheck your credit and shop around pre-approval from multiple lenders (credit unions, banks, online, and dealer financing).
Compare total cost — A low rate on a longer term might cost more overall.
Consider total vehicle price — Negotiate the car price first; financing incentives from manufacturers can sometimes beat bank rates.
Refinance later if rates drop or your credit improves.
Use rate calculators on sites like Bankrate, LendingTree, or lender tools for personalized estimates.

Longer terms (72–84+ months) are increasingly common to keep payments "affordable," but they increase total cost and negative equity risk.Tips for Managing or Lowering PaymentsShop around — Get pre-approved from credit unions, banks, and online lenders (often better than dealer financing).
Larger down payment — Reduces the financed amount significantly.
Shorter term if possible — Balances lower total cost with manageable payments.
Negotiate vehicle price first — The out-the-door price matters most.
Consider used or certified pre-owned — Lower average payments and prices.
Refinance later if rates drop or your credit improves.
