Thoughts

EBT refers to Electronic Benefits Transfer cards used for two main U.S. welfare programs: SNAP (Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program, formerly food stamps) for food purchases and cash assistance programs like TANF (Temporary Assistance for Needy Families, called MFIP in Minnesota).The specific figures of "$1,000 in EBT" (SNAP/food) + "$700 in EBT cash" are not a fixed monthly payment given exclusively or automatically to "Somalians." They represent plausible benefits for a qualifying low- or no-income household of roughly 4–5 people in a high-benefit state like Minnesota, after standard calculations. Benefits are means-tested and vary by household size, income, expenses (e.g., rent, utilities), and state rules.

A ~$1,000 SNAP benefit fits a 4-person household with minimal countable income or a slightly larger one with some deductions. Average benefits are lower nationally (~$200–$300+ per person depending on household).

A ~$700 cash figure aligns with a family of 3–4 with little/no other income. TANF/MFIP has work requirements, time limits (federal lifetime cap of 60 months for most adults), and is not unlimited.

These add up for larger, very low-income households in generous states. The viral claim likely comes from anecdotes or videos of specific Minnesota families (often larger Somali immigrant households) or approximations.Who Qualifies? Same Rules for EveryoneEligibility is not based on ethnicity, nationality, or being "Somali." It is based on:U.S. citizenship or qualified non-citizen status (lawful permanent residents after a 5-year bar in most cases; refugees and asylees historically had faster access and exemptions, though 2025 federal changes tightened rules for some new humanitarian entrants).
Income limits (gross income generally under 130% of federal poverty level; net under 100%).
Asset limits (usually low, e.g., a few thousand dollars).
Work requirements (especially for able-bodied adults without dependents).
Household composition (people who live and eat together).

Any low-income American citizen or qualifying immigrant can apply and receive benefits if they meet the criteria. Millions of native-born Americans do—SNAP serves tens of millions nationwide across all groups. Undocumented immigrants are generally ineligible for these federal benefits (though U.S.-citizen children in mixed households can qualify). 

cwsglobal.org

Why not every American gets this? It is a targeted anti-poverty program, not universal welfare or UBI. Most Americans have incomes, assets, or work situations above the thresholds. Some eligible people do not apply due to bureaucracy, stigma, or other reasons. States vary widely in TANF generosity and rules—some pay far less than Minnesota.Why Higher Reported Usage in Somali Immigrant Communities (Especially Minnesota)?Minnesota has the largest Somali diaspora in the U.S. Data from the American Community Survey (2014–2023, via Center for Immigration Studies analysis) shows:Somali-headed households in Minnesota: ~54% receive SNAP/food stamps (vs. ~7% native-headed households).
~27% receive cash welfare (vs. ~6% native).
~73% have someone on Medicaid (vs. ~18% native).
Overall, ~81% of Somali households use some form of welfare (cash, SNAP, or Medicaid); ~89% for those with children.
Rates remain high (~78% any welfare) even for households with the head in the U.S. 10+ years. 

cis.org

Reasons for the disparity (group-level patterns, not individual or conspiracy):Higher average poverty and lower household incomes, linked to lower average educational attainment (e.g., ~39% of working-age Somali adults with no high school diploma in the data), English/language barriers, and skills from a low-development origin country.
Larger average household sizes (~4 people for Somali households in MN data vs. U.S. average ~2.5), which raises maximum benefit caps.
Refugee/asylee background for many: Initial resettlement often includes benefit navigation and case management.
Concentration in Minnesota, a state with high TANF benefits and possibly higher program outreach/participation.
Cultural/family structure factors (higher fertility, household composition) that increase eligibility.

These are empirical socioeconomic patterns from Census data. Similar patterns appear in other immigrant groups from low-human-capital regions. Native-born poor Americans also qualify and receive benefits when they meet the same rules—many Black and Hispanic households have higher proportional SNAP participation nationally than White households, but absolute numbers are largest for Whites due to population size. 

pewresearch.org

No special program or racial preference exists for Somalis. Rules are the same. Higher usage reflects higher eligibility rates driven by need and demographics. Claims of exclusive access ignore that the programs are means-tested and available to all qualifiers. Notable fraud cases (e.g., Feeding Our Future in Minnesota) have involved individuals from various backgrounds, including Somali-linked networks, but fraud is a separate enforcement issue, not program design.In short: These benefits help low-income households meeting strict criteria. The amounts cited are realistic for larger qualifying families in generous states. Disparities in usage track real group differences in poverty and family size, not favoritism. All Americans who qualify have the same access—many simply do not meet the income/asset/work tests or choose not to participate. For official details, check USDA for SNAP or your state DHS (e.g., Minnesota DHS for MFIP).
