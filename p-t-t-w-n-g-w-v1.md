That's the classic payoff profile of buying options, not selling them. Your statement flips the typical experience of option sellers.Corrected Risk-RewardOption buyers (long calls/puts):
They pay a small premium upfront.  If wrong → lose only the small premium (limited risk).  
If right (big move in the expected direction) → can win big (unlimited/upside leverage).
This is why buyers often seem to "pay cheaply" for lottery-ticket-like asymmetry. The catch: most options expire worthless, so buyers are usually wrong and bleed small amounts repeatedly.

Option sellers (short calls/puts, credit spreads, iron condors, etc.):
They collect a small premium upfront.  If right (option expires worthless or decays) → keep the premium as profit (win small, high probability).  
If wrong (big adverse move) → can lose big (potentially unlimited on naked options).
Sellers are "picking up nickels in front of a steamroller." It feels easy until the rare blow-up.

Your phrasing ("sell options cheaply... think they will never go wrong") matches how many retail traders approach selling. They love the high win rate and steady small credits, underestimating tail risk. But the actual payoff you described at the end matches buying.Why This Dynamic ExistsMarkets price options with implied volatility (IV) that often overestimates realized volatility. Sellers benefit from:Theta decay (time erosion favors sellers).
IV crush after events.
The fact that big moves are rarer than people fear.

But when black swans or earnings surprises hit, sellers get hurt badly. This is why professional market makers and funds sell vol but hedge dynamically, while retail sellers often blow up accounts.Historical examples:2008, 2020 crashes: Naked/short put sellers wiped out.
Meme stock squeezes (GME 2021): Short call sellers destroyed.
Calm bull markets: Sellers print money for months/years.

Practical TakeawaysIf you're selling options:Define risk (use spreads, not naked).
Size small (1-2% of capital per trade max).
Avoid earnings, FOMC, etc., unless hedged.
Track your "win small, lose big" stats rigorously.

If you're buying:You need directional edge + catalyst, or you're just donating premium to sellers.

This asymmetry is why the options market exists—buyers pay for protection/leverage, sellers provide it and get compensated for risk.
