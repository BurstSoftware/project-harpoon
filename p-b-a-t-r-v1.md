What happens when you add B, BB and BBB bonds, and call it highly diversified, and the rating agencies give it a 92- 95 triple A rating without asking any questions

Thoughts

This describes the core mechanism behind mezzanine ABS CDOs (Collateralized Debt Obligations backed by Asset-Backed Securities), which played a central role in amplifying and triggering the 2007–2009 Global Financial Crisis. 

en.wikipedia.org

The Process: Turning Risky Bonds into “Safe” AAA SecuritiesFinancial institutions took lower-rated tranches (primarily BBB, but also BB and even B-rated mezzanine slices) from subprime mortgage-backed securities (RMBS). These were already the riskier portions of pools of subprime home loans.They pooled hundreds of these risky tranches from many different RMBS deals into a new special-purpose vehicle (a CDO). The CDO was then sliced into new tranches with a “waterfall” payment structure:Senior tranches got paid first.
Junior/equity tranches absorbed losses first.

Rating agencies (Moody’s, S&P, Fitch) applied their models and assigned AAA ratings to 70–80% or more of the new CDO’s tranches — even though the underlying collateral was mostly BBB-rated (the lowest investment-grade or just below). 

en.wikipedia.org

They justified this by claiming the portfolio was “highly diversified” across many originators, geographies, and borrowers, so defaults would be uncorrelated and losses contained to junior tranches.In practice:A typical structure might show ~80% AAA on top of a pool of lower-rated bonds/loans/ABS.
You could literally take $100 million in BBB subprime tranches, put them in a CDO, and have two-thirds of the resulting securities rated AAA (with another ~10% AA).

Why the Ratings Were FlawedRating agencies used models (notably the Gaussian copula) that assumed low correlations between defaults. They did not adequately stress-test for a nationwide housing downturn where all mortgages would suffer together due to falling home prices, rising unemployment, and lax origination standards (no-doc loans, etc.).Agencies faced conflicts of interest: They were paid by the issuers (banks) who wanted high ratings to sell the product. Structured finance became hugely profitable for them. 

en.wikipedia.org

The “highly diversified” label ignored systemic risk — everything was tied to the same U.S. housing market.What Actually HappenedWhen U.S. home prices peaked in 2006 and then fell sharply:Subprime delinquencies and defaults exploded (especially adjustable-rate loans).
Losses hit the CDO waterfall hard. Junior tranches were wiped out first, then mezzanine, and eventually senior AAA tranches took massive hits because correlations spiked.

Results:Massive, rapid downgrades: By end-2008, ~91% of CDO securities had been downgraded. Over half of the AAA-rated CDOs issued 2005–2007 (worth ~$300 billion) were impaired (written down or downgraded to junk) by 2009. 

en.wikipedia.org

Enormous losses: CDOs accounted for over $542 billion of the nearly $1 trillion in losses suffered by financial institutions worldwide from 2007 to early 2009. 

en.wikipedia.org

Many “AAA” CDO tranches lost 80–100% of their value.
Banks, insurers, pension funds, and others holding these (or insuring them via credit default swaps) suffered catastrophic mark-to-market losses.

This triggered a liquidity crisis: No one trusted the ratings or the counterparties. Credit markets froze. Major institutions collapsed or needed bailouts (Lehman Brothers, Bear Stearns, AIG, etc.). The result was the worst financial crisis since the Great Depression and a deep global recession.Broader Impacts and AftermathDemand feedback loop: The ability to turn unsellable BBB tranches into AAA product created insatiable demand for more subprime mortgages, fueling the housing bubble.
Synthetic CDOs (using credit default swaps) amplified exposure even further without needing actual mortgages.
Rating agencies faced intense criticism, lawsuits, and regulatory scrutiny. Reforms followed (including parts of the Dodd-Frank Act).
The CDO market largely collapsed but later revived in other forms (e.g., CLOs backed by corporate loans performed differently).

In short: By pooling correlated risky debt, slapping on a “diversified” label, and securing AAA ratings with insufficient scrutiny, the system created the illusion of safety around extremely dangerous assets. When the correlated defaults materialized, the losses were not contained — they cascaded through the entire financial system.This was not abstract theory; it was one of the primary engines of the 2008 crisis. The episode remains a textbook case of model risk, incentive misalignment, and the dangers of underestimating tail correlations in structured finance.
